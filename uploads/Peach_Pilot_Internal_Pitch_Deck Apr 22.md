## Slide 1 — The Problem

**Headline:** Most AI transformations are built on a foundation that can't hold them.

**Anchor stat:**
**95%** of enterprise AI pilots deliver no measurable business impact.
*MIT NANDA, State of AI in Business, 2025*

**Three reasons they fail:**

**1. The Learning Gap**
AI tools forget context between sessions, don't retain feedback, don't learn from corrections, and sit outside the systems where work actually happens. MIT: "The core barrier to scaling is not infrastructure, regulation, or talent. It is learning."

**2. Poor Data Foundations**
AI amplifies bad data. It doesn't fix it. Without a clean foundation, models produce outputs the business rightfully refuses to trust or act on, an illusion of intelligence. Gartner: 60% of AI projects will be abandoned through 2026 due to poor data quality.

**3. Human Workflows, Automated**
Processes designed for people, wrapped in AI instead of redesigned for it. Consultants spend more than 60% of their time gathering information rather than solving problems. Clients pay premium rates to be re-learned every time.

**Closing line:**
Leaders are stuck, afraid, and don't know where to start.

### Speaker Notes

**Opening frame.** The default AI playbook is to start with agent automation. Pick a workflow, build an agent, ship it. The 95% failure rate is not a model problem. Agents are being built on a foundation that cannot support them.

**1. The Learning Gap.** This is the dominant finding in the 2025 MIT NANDA research on enterprise AI. 63% of enterprise buyers now demand context retention as a purchase criterion. The market knows what's missing. The problem compounds: the context the AI needs often lives in employees' heads and consultants' notebooks. When they leave, the institutional memory leaves with them. The company is stuck re-learning itself on every engagement and every new hire.

**2. Poor Data Foundations.** Informatica's 2025 CDO survey found 43% of organizations cite data quality and readiness as their #1 obstacle to AI success. Fragmented, inconsistent, ungoverned data produces outputs that look sophisticated but are fundamentally wrong. The World Economic Forum called it "an illusion of intelligence." Most companies underestimate how long it takes to get data to a place where AI can actually use it. Months of integration, cleanup, and governance work happen before the first agent ships, and the transformation budget is often spent before the transformation begins.

**3. Human Workflows, Automated.** This is the most common failure mode in practice. Companies take processes designed around human memory, attention span, and working hours, and wrap automation around them. That produces a faster version of the wrong thing, not a transformation. McKinsey's 2025 research found that organizations achieving real AI returns were twice as likely to have redesigned workflows before selecting a model. Gartner echoes it: rethinking workflows with AI from the ground up is the ideal path to successful implementation.

**The consulting backstop is also broken.** The industry's default answer to complex transformation is consulting. Enterprises spend over $300 billion a year on it, and the institutional memory of prior engagements is effectively zero. (JP James, Peach Pilot PRD v2.1)

**The result.** Leaders end up in three places at once. Stuck (pilots stall before production). Afraid (governance and risk paralyze adoption). Unsure how to start (no internal capability, no partner that leaves anything compounding behind).

### Speaker Notes

**Opening frame.** Each of the three failure modes on the previous slide has the same root cause. The company was never made legible to AI in the first place. The Company Brain is how we fix that. It is the central system that every agent, workflow, and decision draws from, and the thing that makes the transformation compound instead of reset.

**What the Company Brain is.** Two layers working together. The Knowledge Library is the storage layer. It holds six types of information about the business: company knowledge, domain knowledge, operational signal, strategic context, market intelligence, and a library of expert agents. The Reasoning Engine is the computation layer. It reasons over what the library holds, in two modes. Strategic reasoning for high-stakes deliberative decisions, like which capability to redesign next or whether to change commission structure. Operational reasoning for fast continuous decisions, like which lead goes to which agent, which script to recommend, which policies to flag as cancellation risk.

**Humans in the loop.** The Company Brain is not a replacement for people. Our Forward Deployed Engineers embed with the client to run Discovery, capture context, build the foundation, and redesign capabilities. Client executives stay accountable for strategic decisions. End users work alongside the agents. Humans move to the edges where judgment, taste, and accountability belong. AI runs the middle.

**1. Learns your company.** This directly answers the learning gap. We spend the first weeks of every engagement learning how the business actually runs. Workflows, systems, roles, historical performance, and the strategic context the CEO has never written down. It all gets captured in the Knowledge Library and persists across sessions, engagements, and employees. When a top performer leaves, their context stays. When a new agent joins, they inherit everything the business knows.

**2. Unifies raw and human context.** This answers the poor data foundations problem. Most of what matters in a business is not in a structured database. It is in call recordings, email threads, Slack conversations, meeting notes, and CRM comments. We ingest both. Structured data and unstructured human context get unified into one foundation agents can reason over. No more fragmented inputs. No more AI making confident decisions on half the picture.

**3. Redesigns workflows from first principles, not human habits.** This answers the human workflows automated problem. We do not ask how to add AI to what you do today. We ask how you would design this function if it did not exist and you had AI agents plus humans. The answer is usually not a faster version of the current process. It is a different process.

**How the three come together.** The Company Brain is not the product we sell. It is the foundation we build for every client so that the capabilities we deploy on top of it can actually work. Capabilities without a Company Brain are the 95% failure case. Capabilities built on top of a Company Brain are the 5% success case.

## Slide 3a — The Platform: Company Brain

**Headline:** Two layers. One foundation.

**Subhead:** The Knowledge Library stores what the company knows. The Reasoning Engine decides what to do about it.

**Knowledge Library (storage)**
Six types of information that make the business legible to AI:

1. **Company Knowledge.** How your company actually operates.
2. **Domain Knowledge.** Industry rules, regulations, carrier requirements.
3. **Operational Signal.** Live data from the business heartbeat.
4. **Strategic Context.** The CEO's principles, goals, and guardrails.
5. **Market Intelligence.** What is changing outside your walls.
6. **Expert Agent Library.** Specialized AI agents with deep domain expertise.

**Reasoning Engine (computation)**
Two modes that turn knowledge into decisions:

- **Strategic Reasoning.** High stakes, deliberative. Humans approve.
- **Operational Reasoning.** Fast, continuous, pattern matching. Humans review exceptions.

**Closing line:**
Every capability we build runs on this foundation.

### Speaker Notes

**Opening frame.** The Company Brain is one system with two layers. Storage on one side, computation on the other. Neither works without the other. A library with no reasoner is a filing cabinet. A reasoner with no library is a guess.

**Knowledge Library, the six types.**

*Company Knowledge.* How this specific client actually operates. Org structure, roles, workflows, systems in use, historical performance. Captured during Discovery through what we call the Company X-Ray. This is client-owned data.

*Domain Knowledge.* Industry knowledge that transfers across clients in the same vertical. Insurance regulations by state, carrier underwriting criteria, compliance rules, best practices. Peach Pilot owns this as cross-client IP. The second insurance agency inherits everything we learned at the first.

*Operational Signal.* The continuous heartbeat of the business. Call recordings, close rates, cancellation rates, agent performance, lead quality, scorecard evaluations. This is the honest signal. The data that cannot lie because it is generated by real work happening.

*Strategic Context.* What the CEO actually cares about. Growth targets, risk tolerance, commission philosophy, three year goals, quarterly objectives, what the company will and will not do. Most of this is not written down anywhere. We surface it and capture it.

*Market Intelligence.* External signal. Competitor moves, regulatory changes, carrier policy updates, market trends. Shared across clients where relevant.

*Expert Agent Library.* Specialized AI agents with deep expertise across business strategy, investing strategy, process optimization, change management, and more. Originally built by JP at Hive. Peach Pilot maintains and deploys them per client need.

**Data ownership is explicit.** Client owns Company Knowledge, Operational Signal, and Strategic Context. Peach Pilot owns Domain Knowledge and the Expert Agent Library. Market Intelligence is shared.

**Reasoning Engine, the two modes.**

*Strategic Reasoning.* High stakes, deliberative decisions. Should we change our commission structure? Which capability do we transform next? The engine assembles multiple expert agents and humans, evaluates options against the Knowledge Library, and produces documented recommendations with reasoning. Humans approve before action is taken.

*Operational Reasoning.* Routine, fast, continuous decisions. Route this lead to agent A. Recommend script 7 for this call. Flag this policy as cancellation risk. These run constantly within defined rules. Humans review exceptions, not every decision. Every output is logged and auditable.

**How it gets smarter over time.** On day one the Reasoning Engine is thin. It scores calls, routes leads, recommends scripts. As Operational Signal accumulates, both modes get smarter. By month six it can predict which agent-lead pairings close and stick. By year one it proactively flags patterns humans would have missed.

**Why this is different.** Most AI tools do one or the other. A knowledge base, or an agent. We build both together so they stay in sync. The agents reason over the library. The library captures what the agents learn. The loop closes.

**The eight capabilities in insurance.** These eight surfaced during discovery at Safe Life. They map to the core operating motions of a life insurance agency.

Each capability comes with a pre-built library of skills, templates, best practices, and default configurations. When we deploy Sales and Closing at the second insurance agency, we are not starting from scratch. We are configuring a product we already own. By agency ten, most of the work is configuration, not construction. Cost to deliver drops. Price holds. Margins expand.

**Principle 3: Operated.** This is the piece that separates us from consulting and from SaaS. We do not build the capability and leave. We do not sell software and walk away. Once a capability is live, we run it. System monitoring, AI output quality audits, escalation handling, Reasoning Engine tuning, Knowledge Library maintenance. This is the MSP model. We own the outcome.

**How this scales beyond insurance.** The eight insurance capabilities are not the universe. Every vertical has its own set. ### Speaker Notes

**Opening frame.** Most AI transformations fail because they skip straight to agent deployment. We do the opposite. We spend the first weeks learning the business cold before any agent gets deployed into production. That sequencing is the methodology. It is also why our transformations land where others stall.

**Phase 1 in detail (Weeks 1 to 6).**

*Discovery (Weeks 1 to 2).* We connect to the client's systems, inventory their data, and sit down with leadership to understand their goals, challenges, and how their business actually runs day to day. We capture everything. The systems, the processes, the tribal knowledge, the things that are not written down anywhere.

*Capability Redesign and Deployment.* We redesign the highest-impact business function from first principles using our AI agents. For insurance, this might be automated call scoring with coaching, intelligent carrier matching, or lead routing optimization. Everything deployed with human in the loop governance.

*Expand.* Each subsequent capability benefits from a richer Company Brain. The platform learns from every interaction. Delivery gets faster and cheaper with each cycle.

**Why the 92 agents number matters.** The 92 prebuilt agents are not written from scratch at every engagement. They are a library we maintain and configure per client. That is what productization looks like in practice. A new engagement is not a new build. It is a new configuration.

**Why humans in the loop is not optional.** Governance is not a feature we add later. It is built into the methodology from phase one. Strategic decisions route through human approval. Operational decisions run within defined rules and escalate exceptions. Every output is logged and auditable. This is how we move fast without breaking the business.

**How this compounds.** Engagement one is the hardest. We build the X-Ray template, refine the Company Brain schema, productize the first capability. Engagement two inherits all of that. ## Slide 5 — We Own The Outcome, Not The Software

**Headline:** We get paid when your numbers move.

**Subhead:** A commercial model built around shared outcomes, not licensed software or billable hours.

**1. Transformation Fee.**

Outcome-based. Tied to measurable improvements over baseline. Close rate up, chargebacks down, ramp time faster, retention higher.

**Why this works:**

- **Consulting firms sell hours.** They have no skin in the outcome.
- **Software companies sell licenses.** They do not operate the capability.
- **We do both.** Build it, run it, get paid on the result.

**Closing line:**
If the system does not work, we do not eat.

### Speaker Notes

**Opening frame.** This slide is the commercial thesis of the entire company. Every other slide explains what we do. This one explains how the business makes money and why the incentive structure is unlike anything in the market.

**The Emergence Capital AINS framework.** Emergence Capital mapped AI pricing on two axes. Autonomy (how independently the AI operates) and attribution (how clearly the value can be measured). We target the upper-right quadrant. High autonomy because the AI scores every call, routes every lead, and generates coaching without human intervention. High attribution because close rates, chargeback rates, and agent ramp times are all directly measurable. The service is the outcome. There is no attribution problem to argue about.

**The three components in sequence.**

*Transformation Fee.* Fixed scope, fixed price. Covers the first weeks of work: Discovery, loading the Brain, delivering the X-Ray, and redesigning the first capability. Priced at market-norm consulting rates during the learning phase. As the methodology automates, our cost to deliver drops but the price holds. Margins expand.

*Managed Operations Retainer.* Once a capability is live, we run it. This is the MSP function. Monthly retainer covers system monitoring, AI output quality audits, escalation handling, Reasoning Engine tuning, and Knowledge Library maintenance. Priced per active capability per month plus a scale metric (like number of agents). This is the base recurring revenue and it covers our cost to operate.

*Performance Component.* The premium layer. Tied to measurable improvements over baseline. At Safe Life this looks like: a percentage of incremental revenue above baseline close rate, a share of saved commissions when chargebacks drop, a bonus for compressing time-to-first-3-deals below historical average, and a bonus tied to agent retention. This is layered in roughly 90 days after capability deployment, once baseline metrics are established.

**Why each alternative model cannot do this.**

*Consulting firms* are built on billable hours. Their incentive is to extend the engagement, not end it. They cannot sign an outcome-based contract without destroying their economics. Their infrastructure, bench, and pricing are all built around time and materials.

*Software companies* are built on licenses and seats. They sell access to a tool. They do not run the workflow. They cannot guarantee an outcome because they do not operate the capability that produces it. The client still has to figure out how to use the software.

*We are built for this.* Our unit of delivery is a capability, not an hour or a license. Our team is structured around building, running, and improving capabilities in production. The outcome-based component is not an add-on. It is the reason the whole model works.

**Why this is also a moat.** This is the deepest structural moat we have. A consulting firm cannot match it without rebuilding their entire business. A software company cannot match it without taking on operational risk they are not built to carry. We had to choose this model on day one. Competitors trying to copy it have to dismantle theirs.
